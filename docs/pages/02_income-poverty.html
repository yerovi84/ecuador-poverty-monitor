Income poverty

Ecuador’s 2025 annual poverty profile

This page turns the annual ENEMDU poverty results into a compact reading of income, territory, and benchmark consistency.

In 2025, Ecuador’s annual income poverty rate reached 21.95%. The national figure is the starting point, but the substantive story is territorial: poverty reached 16.51% in urban areas and 33.63% in rural areas.

What this page shows

This page summarizes Ecuador’s 2025 annual income poverty profile, focusing on the national rate, the urban-rural gap, extreme poverty, and benchmark consistency.

2025 annual reading

Income poverty

The annual estimate shows that roughly one in five people lived in households whose per-capita income did not reach the income poverty line. The national rate was 21.95%.

The monitor treats this figure as an annual structural reading, not as a monthly pulse. That distinction matters because the annual ENEMDU file supports a broader territorial and thematic analysis than short-term releases.

Extreme poverty

A deeper income threshold

Extreme income poverty reached 6.99% nationally in 2025. The territorial contrast is again sharp: 3.69% in urban areas and 14.07% in rural areas.

This layer separates income poverty from extreme income poverty because the two indicators answer different questions. Poverty identifies households below the income poverty line; extreme poverty identifies households below a more restrictive minimum threshold.

Derived insights

What the gaps add

Income poverty is 17.12 percentage points higher in rural areas than in urban areas. In relative terms, the rural rate is about 2.04 times the urban rate.

Extreme poverty is 10.38 percentage points higher in rural areas. The rural extreme-poverty rate is about 3.81 times the urban rate, which means the territorial divide is even steeper at the deepest income threshold.

Population profiles

Who is more exposed to income poverty?

The annual profile shows that income poverty is not evenly distributed across population groups. Children and adolescents have the highest poverty rate in this first profile layer, and adult education shows a clear gradient: poverty falls as educational attainment rises.

The sex difference is much smaller than the age and education gradients, which keeps the interpretation focused on structural exposure rather than small headline differences.

Extreme poverty follows the same vulnerability pattern

Selected profile estimates point in the same direction: extreme poverty reaches 10.36% for children and adolescents, 11.23% among adults with no formal education or literacy center, and 2.11% among adults with higher education.

Source and interpretation

These profiles are survey-weighted analytical estimates derived from annual ENEMDU microdata. They are not directly benchmarked against public annual tabulations, so they should be read as analytical profiles rather than benchmark-validated headline estimates.

Source and reproducibility

How the evidence is grounded

The headline estimates are computed from public ENEMDU annual microdata and compared with public annual INEC tabulations. The repository documents the processing workflow and generated outputs for reproducibility. This comparison is not an official institutional validation.

The page shows aggregated results only. It does not expose survey files, household records, person records, local paths, or private data locations.

Interpretation limits

What this page does not claim

This page does not estimate causality, explain every driver of poverty, or create a new official statistic. It presents a stable annual analytical reading based on aggregated results.

Distributional analysis will be added once quintile or income-distribution outputs are available. The next versions of the monitor should add uncertainty, representativity notes, and deeper territorial detail only where the survey design supports those domains.

Next analytical layers

What comes after the first layer

The next income-poverty layer can add poverty-line context, precision indicators, historical comparisons, and distributional readings. Deprivation, multidimensional poverty, labor, and province-level analysis should remain separate layers until their own publication rules are complete.
