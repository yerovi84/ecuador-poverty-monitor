Core idea

Why the frame matters

A sampling frame is the operational map from which a household survey draws its sample. In plain language, it defines the population coverage, organizes the units that can be selected, supports known probabilities of selection, and shapes what domains can be estimated with useful precision.

This is why the sampling frame is not a backstage detail. It affects the relationship between the survey sample and the population it is meant to represent. Good formulas and careful weighting still need a frame that reflects where households are and how the population is distributed.

Frame functions

Four things a frame does

Coverage

Defines who can enter the survey operation and which households are reachable through the design.

Selection

Organizes the stages and units used to select households under a probability sample.

Domains

Conditions which national, urban, rural, territorial or labor domains can be interpreted responsibly.

ENEMDU over time

ENEMDU and the challenge of time

INEC’s sampling-design documentation explains that the ENEMDU sample frame is based on the final results and cartography of Ecuador’s 2010 Population and Housing Census, with partial cartographic updates and later adjustments to improve the construction of primary sampling units. The same documentation notes that population growth, decline and housing changes can alter how balanced those units are over time.

That statement should be read carefully. It does not mean that ENEMDU estimates are invalid. It means that a household survey frame is a living statistical infrastructure. Demographic change, migration, urban expansion and territorial change make documentation and modernization important for interpreting poverty, employment and territorial evidence.

International guidance

The frame is part of survey quality

The United Nations guide on household survey samples treats sampling frames and master samples as central pieces of household survey design. It links frame quality with probability sampling, domains, sample design documentation, weights and sampling errors.

The practical lesson is simple: household surveys are strongest when the frame, sample design, weighting, fieldwork and public documentation are read together. A static result table cannot carry that full context by itself.

Poverty and labor

Why this matters for poverty and labor

Income poverty, deprivation, territorial poverty and labor-market indicators all depend on who is represented by the survey design. If population change concentrates in areas or groups that are harder to reach, the frame and its updates become part of the interpretation.

For poverty, the concern is not only the national rate. It is also whether urban, rural, provincial or subgroup readings are supported by the design. For labor, the concern extends to employment quality, subemployment, informality and people outside the labor force, because these groups can be unevenly distributed across geography and household types.

This monitor therefore treats domain reliability as part of the public story. More detailed disaggregation is useful only when the sampling frame, sample size and survey design support that level of reading.

Claim boundary

What this monitor can and cannot claim

This monitor does not audit INEC, does not replace official statistics, and does not correct the official sampling frame. It is a downstream analytical communication layer built from public information.

What it can do is make the role of survey design visible. Public microdata analysis should be read together with coverage, domain and representativity limits, especially when moving from national indicators to territorial or labor subgroup profiles.

Modernization

A sober modernization agenda

Modernization is best framed as statistical maintenance, not as accusation. A prudent agenda would update household survey frames with more recent census and cartographic information, document the timing and scope of updates, evaluate territorial and labor domains, preserve comparable time series where possible, and publish clear metadata about changes.

That kind of transparency helps both official producers and downstream users. It lets readers distinguish real social change from design changes, sampling uncertainty or domain limits.

Reading note

This annex is methodological. It does not calculate new indicators, open microdata, assess field operations, or certify any estimate. It explains why poverty and labor interpretation should include the frame, the survey design and the domain of estimation.
