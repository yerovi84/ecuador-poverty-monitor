Public question

Why having work does not always mean being out of poverty

Poverty is measured through household per-capita income, not only through whether a person has a job. That distinction is crucial: a person can be employed and still live in a household whose total income is below the income-poverty line.

The national profile shows that poverty among employed people is 15.9%, while poverty among unemployed people is 25.8% and poverty among people outside the labor force is 21.9%. The estimated number of people in poverty is largest among employed people, at about 1,317,829.

Among employed people, adequate/full employment reaches 38.5% and subemployment reaches 20.1%. This is why the labor layer looks beyond the employed/unemployed split: job quality changes how employment should be read alongside household income poverty.

Unemployment is associated with a high poverty rate, but it accounts for a smaller estimated number of people in poverty than employment. The national estimate shows about 1,039,215 people in poverty outside the labor force, another reminder that household vulnerability extends beyond the labor-force headline.

Reading notes

These estimates are analytical and descriptive. They are based on public ENEMDU annual 2025 microdata and are published here only through a curated aggregate output. They are not an official INEC validation, do not make causal claims, and should be read together with Measurement and statistical quality.
